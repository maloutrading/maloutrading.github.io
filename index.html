the three rules

01

cut your losses

Losses work diametrically against the speculator: a 10% loss needs about 11% gain just to get back to break-even. A 50% loss already demands a 100% gain for the same. Anyone who does not cut losses consistently runs into trouble sooner or later.

02

never add to losers

Trading can hurt. Never add to a losing position.

03

don't trade against the trend

The trend is the speculator's tailwind. Trading against it means swimming against the current - possible, but costly and rarely lasting. Go with the market, not against it; the biggest moves reward those who wait patiently on the right side.

1DNA chip writes genetic code

A team at the Karolinska Institute is building a silicon chip that writes dozens of DNA strands at once, using only electricity and water-soluble enzymes.

DNA stores data millions of times denser than any hard drive and lasts for millennia. A portable writer moves this out of the clean-room lab toward everyday life - turning the blueprint of life into a writable commodity.

- Karolinska Institutet

2EU rules slow down Siri

Apple is delaying new voice-assistant features in the EU, citing the Digital Markets Act as the reason.

The dispute raises an uncomfortable question: does Europe's openness come at the price of new technology arriving later here? Every big platform is watching whether delay pays off as leverage against regulation.

- Reuters

3Pesticide hits bumblebees in the genome

Sulfoxaflor, the neonicotinoid successor considered safe, disrupts bumblebee reproduction even in tiny doses, according to a study.

Pollinators are the quiet infrastructure of agriculture. If the replacement carries the same harm under a new name, food gets more expensive long before anyone names the cause.

- Nature

4United Nations move to govern AI

In Geneva, a joint body of the United Nations and the telecommunications union is launching to draft the first global guardrails for artificial intelligence.

The world's regions are currently writing incompatible AI rulebooks. Whoever sets a standard first shapes it for everyone else - the Geneva table arrives late to a race that has long been running.

- United Nations

5Atlantic current more stable than thought

New measurement series on the great Atlantic overturning find, alongside signs of weakening, surprising stability as well.

The current keeps Europe's winters mild and was thought to be at risk of tipping. If the data is less settled than assumed, that shifts one of the loudest tipping-point narratives of recent years.

- Woods Hole Oceanographic Institution

critical thinking

The DNA-chip story (1) invites base-rate neglect: the reflex of inferring near-term everyday use from a single lab success. The vast majority of such breakthroughs quietly fizzle on the long road out of the lab, and no one reports on that fizzling. With every breakthrough headline, ask: how many similar announcements of recent years actually reached real people?

Fourier analysis

The Fourier analysis is the proof that every sound, however tangled, is made of pure, simple tones - just as white light splits into its colors in a prism. The rush of the sea, an orchestra, an earthquake: all of it can be resolved into a handful of hidden oscillations, each with its own pitch and strength. The brilliant core is that complicated reality need not stay complicated once you find the right language to describe it. Today this principle sits in every phone: it breaks your voice into frequencies, throws away the inaudible ones and transmits only the rest - so a conversation fits through a narrow line. The climate researchers from story 5 likewise search noisy ocean data for such hidden rhythms. What looks chaotic is often just a sum of simple waves that no one noticed on their own.

contested thesis

The greatest danger of artificial intelligence is not that machines become too clever, but that we stop thinking for ourselves.

The debate stares at distant scenarios of a superior machine, yet the real shift is quiet and already underway: whoever gets answers at the push of a button practices hard self-thinking less often, and what you do not practice withers - from mental arithmetic to a sense of direction. A society that outsources its judgment is made vulnerable not by an uprising of machines, but by its own convenience.

against it: every technology from the printing press to the pocket calculator was once seen as intellectual decay - and each time thinking merely moved to a higher level instead of disappearing.

prediction market

Of three political markets, one stands out: the probability of a ceasefire in an ongoing conflict jumped from 31 to 58 percent within twenty days. The crowd reacted to diplomatic signals that surfaced in the big headlines only days later - a lesson in how betting markets are occasionally faster than the newsrooms. At the same time the herd instinct shows: the strongest jump came not with the news itself, but once the crowd saw that everyone else was already buying.

on the rise

Wikipedia page views for the term perovskite solar cell have shot up since yesterday. The trigger is a new study reporting an efficiency close to the theoretical limit for these thin-film cells. Perovskites are considered cheaper and more flexible than classic silicon; the attention shows that the public senses a possible turn in solar technology before the mass media proclaim it.
